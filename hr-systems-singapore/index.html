Operational Systems

HR systems for
the operational reality.

People operations tends to run across forms, spreadsheets, inboxes and informal follow-up. That works until it does not — usually at the moment someone needs an answer quickly and there is no single place to look.

Where it breaks

HR carries processes that are simultaneously routine and sensitive: requests, approvals, allocation, documentation. When those live in email, the record exists only in someone's memory, and consistency depends on who happened to handle it.

The aim is not to replace an HRIS. It is to give the operational layer around it — the requests, the approvals, the resourcing decisions — a structure people will actually use.

What gets built

01

Resource allocation

Staffing needs, assignments, capacity and change history tracked across teams, projects or sites.

02

Approvals

Standardised requests, review steps, documentation and escalation — consistent regardless of who is handling them.

03

Visibility

Workload, status and exceptions surfaced for the people accountable, without another manual return.

Commonly relevant to

  • Organisations deploying people across shifts, sites or projects, where allocation is a daily decision
  • HR functions holding policy-sensitive processes with no reliable audit trail
  • Teams whose headcount planning lives in a spreadsheet that quietly diverges from reality
  • Leaders who cannot answer “who is working on what, and at what capacity” without asking around

The practice is based in Singapore, where most operational systems work is delivered. Engagements also run across Japan and the wider region — the operating problems rarely respect a border.

Related work

HR approvals are workflow with higher sensitivity — the design principles are shared with workflow automation. Where the requirement is leadership-level visibility over workload and exceptions, that is governance dashboard territory.
